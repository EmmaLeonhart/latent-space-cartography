Beyond Proximity: Mapping the Topology of Isosymbolic Space

Working draft — forthcoming arXiv submission

Abstract

Standard Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) retrieves context by vector proximity — cosine similarity between a query embedding and a corpus of document embeddings. This works well for topical retrieval but fails systematically on multi-hop causal reasoning, where essential intermediate premises may be semantically distant from the query. We propose a neurosymbolic architecture that constructs a Virtual Knowledge Graph (VKG) at runtime from propositional extractions and runs a logic engine over it, retrieving reasoning chains through entity bridging rather than vector similarity alone.

Probing the geometry of the embedding space suggests that it is not a uniform representational medium but a heterogeneous manifold that exhibits three empirically distinguishable regimes. In low-density, high-entropy regions — which we term undersymbolic space — coordinates fail to support stable symbolic interpretations, and retrieval produces vague or under-specified results. In high-density regions — which we term oversymbolic space — many symbolically distinct propositions are compressed into overlapping coordinates, degrading the ability of vector operations to respect predicate and role distinctions, and retrieval becomes prone to hallucination. Between these extremes lies the isosymbolic space: a regime where local vector operations approximately preserve predicate–argument structure, enabling both logical inference and fast geometric shortcuts. We provide evidence for these regimes: a 3,200× loadedness ratio between the most and least dense regions, smooth but rapid transitions, and characteristic failure modes (predicate blindness, causal chain decay) that cluster near regime boundaries.

This three-regime characterization suggests two complementary applications. The neural → symbolic → neural direction — our VKG architecture — addresses hallucinations by grounding vector retrieval in explicit logical structure. The converse symbolic → neural → symbolic direction is a design implication we have not yet implemented: when a complex logical operation falls entirely within the isosymbolic regime, it could in principle be executed as vector arithmetic rather than graph traversal. A system that can predetermine which regime a query occupies could select the optimal reasoning strategy accordingly.

On a 7-scenario benchmark spanning pharmacology, economics, geophysics, and engineering, the neurosymbolic pipeline achieves 69% mean ground-truth coverage versus 62% for standard RAG, with gains of up to +50% on causal cascade scenarios. The two scenarios where standard RAG outperforms are attributable to extraction model failures, not architectural limitations — we do not observe cases where the VKG underperforms standard RAG when provided with well-formed propositions.

1. Introduction

RAG systems retrieve context for language model generation by finding documents (or chunks) whose embeddings are close to the query in vector space. This is effective when the answer is stated directly in a single retrievable passage. But many real-world questions require reasoning across multiple passages — connecting facts through shared entities, causal chains, or logical entailment.

Consider: “At what temperature does water boil on Mount Everest?” The answer requires chaining four facts: (1) Everest is ~8,849m above sea level, (2) atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude, (3) lower pressure reduces boiling point, (4) water boils at ~70°C under those conditions. A standard RAG system retrieves fact (4) easily (high similarity to the query) but misses fact (2), which scores below the distractor “Mount Everest is on the border between Nepal and Tibet” despite being essential to the reasoning chain.

Core thesis: Embedding spaces encode logical structure with non-uniform fidelity, systematically under-representing relational/predicate structure relative to entity/topical structure. Furthermore, as finite-state representations (Section 2.3), they cannot perform unbounded compositional reasoning. We demonstrate this empirically (Section 4) and propose a three-pillar architecture that uses a runtime VKG as a semantic space — complementing the feature space of vector embeddings — to recover the reasoning chains that cosine similarity misses.

More fundamentally, our embedding geometry experiments suggest that the latent space is not a uniform medium but a heterogeneous manifold exhibiting three empirically distinguishable regimes. In undersymbolic regions, coordinates are sparse and fail to support stable symbolic interpretations — retrieval here yields vagueness, not reasoning. In oversymbolic regions, representational density is so high that symbolically distinct propositions are compressed into overlapping coordinates, and vector arithmetic ceases to preserve predicate and role distinctions — a failure mode associated with hallucination. Between these extremes lies the isosymbolic space: a regime where local vector transformations approximately preserve predicate–argument structure. The VKG architecture exploits this characterization by grounding retrieval in explicit symbolic structure when vector operations alone cannot be trusted.

2. Related Work

2.1 Retrieval-Augmented Generation and GraphRAG

RAG (Lewis et al. 2020) augments LLM generation with retrieved passages, typically selected by cosine similarity between query and document embeddings. GraphRAG (Edge et al. 2024) extends this by building a knowledge graph from the corpus and using community detection to identify thematic clusters, enabling multi-document summarization. However, GraphRAG’s graph is entity-centric — nodes are entities, edges are relationships — and retrieval still relies on embedding similarity within detected communities. Our approach differs in two ways: propositions (not entities) are the atomic units, and retrieval is driven by structural connectivity (entity bridging) rather than vector proximity.

2.2 Neurosymbolic AI and Verified Intelligence

Seshia, Sadigh, and Sastry (2022) frame the challenge of verified AI around three pillars: environment modeling, formal specification, and verification algorithms. Our three-pillar architecture (extraction, mapping, logic) parallels this structure, applying it specifically to the retrieval problem. The broader neurosymbolic programme (Garcez et al. 2019; Kautz 2022) seeks to combine the pattern-recognition strengths of neural networks with the consistency guarantees of symbolic reasoning. Our work contributes an empirical case study showing where this combination yields measurable gains in a practical NLP pipeline.

2.3 Transformers as Finite-State Machines

We present a proof sketch establishing that transformer-based language models are, in the strict formal sense, finite-state machines — and are therefore not Turing-complete on their own. This is not merely an abstract observation; it directly explains the failure modes documented in Section 4 and motivates the VKG as an architectural solution.

Theorem. A transformer with fixed parameters is a finite-state transducer.

Step 1: Finite state space. A transformer has L layers, a d-dimensional residual stream, H attention heads per layer, a bounded context window n, and b-bit floating-point precision. The complete internal state at any inference step is determined by the residual stream activations (a matrix in Rn×d) and the attention patterns (L×H matrices in Rn×n). Since all parameters are fixed and finite, the total number of distinguishable internal states is bounded by |S| ≤ 2b×n×d×L — astronomically large but finite. A system with a finite set of internal states and deterministic transitions is, by definition, a finite-state machine.

Step 2: No external memory. A Turing machine requires an unbounded tape. A transformer has no such mechanism: its working memory is the residual stream (fixed at width d and length n), and its long-term memory is its parameters (fixed after training). It cannot dynamically allocate new memory during inference. For any fixed transformer, there exists a logical formula whose evaluation requires more state than the architecture provides.

Step 3: Formal results. Merrill, Sabharwal, and Smith (2022) prove that saturated (hard-attention) transformers are equivalent to constant-depth threshold circuits (the complexity class TC0), which cannot compute general Boolean formula evaluation or recognize context-free languages requiring unbounded sequential computation. Hahn (2020) proves that self-attention cannot recognize Dyck-2 (balanced parentheses with two bracket types) at unbounded depth, establishing a formal expressivity ceiling independent of model size. Pérez, Barceló, and Marinkovic (2021) show that transformers with unbounded precision are Turing-complete — but this requires infinite precision, precisely the condition that does not hold in practice.

Step 4: Chain-of-thought does not escape finiteness. Chain-of-thought prompting unrolls sequential computation into the token dimension, increasing the effective depth from L to L×n. But n is finite, so the system remains finite-state. For any fixed (L, d, n), there exists a reasoning chain of length L×n+1 that cannot be executed.

Corollary for embeddings. An embedding function maps countably infinite text strings into Rd with b-bit precision, yielding at most 2b×d distinguishable points. By the pigeonhole principle, infinitely many distinct strings must collide. Our experiments (Section 4) show that this compression is not uniform: the distributional training signal allocates 3.5× more representational capacity to entities than to predicates. Embeddings do encode logical structure — compositionality is approximately additive, verbs act as consistent displacement vectors — but with systematically non-uniform fidelity.

The VKG as external tape. The Virtual Knowledge Graph is, formally, the external memory that the finite-state transformer lacks. It provides unbounded entity tracking (limited only by corpus size, not architectural parameters), explicit relational structure (predicates as first-class edge labels), and compositional traversal (multi-hop reasoning as graph path-finding rather than fixed-depth forward passes). In the Chomsky hierarchy, augmenting a finite-state machine with an unbounded external tape yields a Turing machine. The VKG is that tape.

2.4 Knowledge Graph Embeddings

TransE (Bordes et al. 2013) models relations as translations in embedding space; RotatE (Sun et al. 2019) uses rotations; ComplEx (Trouillon et al. 2016) uses complex-valued embeddings for asymmetric relations. These methods embed entities and learn relation-specific transformations. Our approach is complementary: we embed entire propositions (preserving contextual integrity) and construct explicit graph structure at runtime rather than learning it from a fixed training set. The VKG is assembled on-the-fly for each query, making it applicable to arbitrary corpora without retraining.

2.5 System 1 / System 2 Cognition

Kahneman (2011) distinguishes fast, intuitive cognition (System 1) from slow, deliberate reasoning (System 2). This framing maps directly onto the neurosymbolic architecture: the embedding model provides System 1 — rapid pattern-matching that retrieves topically relevant passages — while the VKG and logic engine provide System 2 — structured traversal of reasoning chains that the intuitive system misses. Standard RAG relies exclusively on System 1; our architecture adds System 2 as a complementary retrieval pathway.

3. Method: Three-Pillar Architecture

Pillar 1: Propositional Extraction

Each sentence in the corpus is decomposed into (subject, predicate, object) triples via an LLM (currently DeepSeek-R1 8B). Unlike entity-centric KG construction, we embed the entire proposition rather than individual entities — preserving contextual integrity (“Company A acquired Company B in 2022 for the purpose of X” rather than the bare triple [A]→[acquired]→[B]).

Pillar 2: VKG Construction with Entity Bridging

Propositions are embedded (mxbai-embed-large) and placed in both a NetworkX directed graph (for path-finding) and an RDFLib graph (for formal SPARQL queries). Crucially, propositions sharing extracted entities are linked through entity bridging — creating edges that cosine similarity alone would never produce.

Pillar 3: Logic Engine

A multi-hop chain search traverses the VKG to find reasoning paths from query-relevant seed propositions to the answer. Contradiction detection via SPARQL prunes inconsistent chains. The retrieved chain is then provided as context to the generation model.

4. Embedding Geometry: Why Vector Similarity Fails for Reasoning

Before evaluating the full pipeline, we characterize why standard RAG fails on multi-hop queries by probing the geometry of the embedding space.

The internal similarity structure within each verb’s 64 propositions is nearly identical across verbs (mean Pearson r = 0.939). The embedding space treats predicates as translations that shift the entity cluster without deforming it — a kind of finite-state logical operation, systematic but compressed relative to entities.

4.2 The Syllogism Gap

In the classic Socrates syllogism, the two premises — “All men are mortal” and “Socrates is a man” — are the most distant pair in embedding space (0.560), yet they are precisely the two sentences needed together to derive the conclusion. The conclusion “Socrates is mortal” sits near “Socrates is a man” (0.879) because they share an entity, not because the model understands entailment.

To test this systematically, we constructed a 10×10×10 combinatorial grid of syllogisms, all following the uniform template: P1 = “All {class} are {adjective}”, P2 = “{member} is a {class}”, C = “{member} is {adjective}”. Ten unambiguous proper nouns (Socrates, Aristotle, Einstein, Mozart, Shakespeare, Cleopatra, Darwin, Galileo, Confucius, Archimedes) crossed with 10 classes and 10 adjectives yield 1,000 syllogisms from only 300 unique sentences (since P1 depends only on class+adjective, P2 on member+class, C on member+adjective).

A retrieval system cannot perform vector arithmetic — it can only rank by cosine to a query. The gap is not missing information but the wrong access pattern: similarity versus composition.

4.2.2 Proposition Synthesis via Bridge Removal

The regression framing treats syllogistic inference as curve-fitting. But a syllogism is a logical operation: identify the shared entity (bridge), resolve on it, and compose what remains. In embedding space, this corresponds to: (1) detect the bridge entity between two propositions, (2) remove it, (3) compose the residuals.

Bridge detection works. Given P1 and P2 plus the bare word embeddings of member, class, and adjective, the bridge can be identified as the word with the highest minimum cosine similarity to both premises. This simple heuristic correctly identifies the class as the shared entity in 999/1,000 cases (99.9%).

But synthesis from sentence residuals fails. The natural operation — remove the bridge from both premises, compose the residuals — performs poorly. Subtracting the class embedding from P1 does not cleanly isolate the adjective property; it leaves behind the “All … are …” template noise. The residual P1 − K has cosine only 0.115 to the bare adjective (the intended content) and retains 65% of the original variance in template structure. When these noisy residuals are composed, performance drops to 0.385 — worse than P2 alone (0.779). A fitted regression on the residuals confirms this: α(P1 − K) + β(P2 − K) learns α ≈ 0.02 (effectively discarding P1’s residual entirely) while β ≈ 0.54.

Sentence templates — “All … are …”, “… is a …” — are confounds that contaminate the residuals when you try to subtract semantic content from full sentences. This is precisely why the VKG architecture requires Pillar 1 (propositional extraction): extracting (subject, predicate, object) triples strips the syntactic template before Pillar 2’s entity bridging operates on clean semantic components. The embedding space can detect which entities are shared (99.9%), but it cannot compose new propositions from sentence-level residuals without first decomposing into symbolic parts.

4.4 Predicate Blindness Across Domains

Four sentences sharing the predicate “inhibits” across pharmacology, botany, economics, and governance domains have a mean similarity of only 0.450 — barely above random. Identical relational structure is invisible to embeddings when the domains differ.

4.5 No Universal Abstraction Axis

Testing 50 taxonomic hierarchies (20 noun, 20 verb, 10 adjective, 218 unique words — scaled up from 24 hierarchies): there is no single “up” direction in embedding space corresponding to increasing abstraction. Noun hierarchies share moderate agreement (0.476), verb hierarchies less (0.408), adjective hierarchies least (0.157). Cross-POS agreement is near zero (noun×verb = 0.057, adj×noun = 0.022). Only 6/50 hierarchies (12%) show monotonic distance decay from origin to terminal word. Same-abstraction-level words do not cluster: within-level similarity (0.552–0.572) approximately equals cross-level similarity (0.553–0.565). Embedding geometry is distributional, not ontological.

4.6 Distributional vs. Ontological Distance

dog→animal (0.876) scores higher than dog→mammal (0.816), despite mammal being taxonomically closer. Embedding distance reflects co-occurrence frequency, not formal taxonomic distance. Word register matters: “dog” (informal), “canine” (taxonomic), and “carnivoran” (scientific) occupy different regions of the space.

4.7 Linnaean Taxonomy: Register Consistency Does Not Help

One might hypothesize that the distributional confounds in Section 4.6 arise from mixing informal and scientific registers within a single hierarchy (puppy→dog→canine→mammal→animal). We tested this by using purely formal Linnaean taxonomy — Canis lupus familiaris → Canis → Canidae → Carnivora → Mammalia → Vertebrata → Chordata → Animalia → Eukaryota — across 10 organisms.

Result: Only 1/20 Linnaean hierarchies show monotonic distance decay (0/15 for common English names). Scaling from 10 to 20 Linnaean organisms and 6 to 15 common-name organisms confirms the finding: the violation rate is stable at ~2 violations per Linnaean hierarchy and ~3 per common-name hierarchy. The “Animalia bounce” is universal — every animal hierarchy shows Animalia closer to the species name than Chordata is, because “Animalia” appears in far more contexts (legal, philosophical, educational) than “Chordata” (biology textbooks only).

The most extreme case is E. coli: “Bacteria” (0.806) is closer to “Escherichia coli” than “Enterobacterales” (0.567) is — the hierarchy is inverted for the top four levels. This is because “E. coli” and “Bacteria” massively co-occur in general science writing, while “E. coli” and “Enterobacterales” co-occur almost exclusively in taxonomy databases.

Convergence between branches remains literal, not graduated: Canidae ↔ Felidae = 0.785, then instant 1.000 at shared Carnivora. Even the most formally structured taxonomy humans have ever created cannot be recovered from embedding geometry.

4.8 Why Hyperbolic Embeddings Will Not Fix This

A likely objection is that the limitations documented above are artifacts of Euclidean geometry, and that hyperbolic embeddings (Nickel & Kiela 2017) — which have exponentially growing volume with distance from the origin, naturally matching tree branching factors — would resolve the hierarchy recovery problem. Our data argues otherwise.

The non-monotonicity we observe is not geometric but distributional. “Bacteria” is closer to “Escherichia coli” than “Enterobacterales” is not because Euclidean space lacks room for a tree, but because “E. coli” and “Bacteria” co-occur millions of times in PubMed abstracts while “E. coli” and “Enterobacterales” co-occur almost exclusively in taxonomy databases. A hyperbolic model trained on the same corpus would learn the same co-occurrence statistics and place “Bacteria” close to “E. coli” in the Poincaré disk for exactly the same distributional reasons.

The “Animalia bounce” would become the “Animalia warp” — hyperbolic space compresses distances near the origin and stretches them at the boundary, so a word incorrectly pulled toward the origin by co-occurrence frequency would create larger effective tree-distance errors, not smaller ones.

Hyperbolic embeddings show strong results on curated ontologies (WordNet, medical taxonomies) where the training data is the hierarchy itself — (hypernym, hyponym) pairs that give the model exactly the signal it needs. But this is precisely the point: you need the explicit symbolic hierarchy first. The hyperbolic embedding is a compressed representation of an ontology that already exists; it does not discover ontology from distributional data. For general-purpose retrieval over arbitrary text — which is the RAG use case — the problem is not the geometry of the embedding space but the absence of explicit symbolic structure. The VKG adds what is actually missing.

4.9 Dimensional Cramming: A Unifying Theory

The empirical findings of Sections 4.1–4.8 share a common cause. An embedding model must compress the full space of propositional semantics — entities, predicates, quantification, modality, negation — into a fixed number of dimensions. This compression is necessarily lossy, and the loss is not uniform: the model allocates representational capacity according to distributional signal strength in the training corpus, not according to logical importance.

Entities and topics dominate natural language: nouns are frequent, referentially stable, and contextually salient. Predicates are comparatively sparse and context-dependent (“inhibits” appears in pharmacology, botany, economics, and governance with completely different arguments). The result is what we call dimensional cramming: the embedding space devotes the majority of its capacity to entity/topic distinctions (explaining the 3.5× subject advantage in §4.1) while predicates are compressed into a narrow residual subspace (explaining predicate blindness in §4.4).

This is not a flaw in any particular model — it is an optimal allocation for the objective these models are trained on (next-token prediction, contrastive passage retrieval). The training signal rewards placing “dog” near “animal” (high co-occurrence) over placing “inhibits” near “blocks” (same logical role, different domains). The vector space faithfully learns what co-occurs with what, but this is precisely not what entails what.

Viewed through the lens of computational expressivity: a fixed-dimensional embedding is a finite-state representation. It has no mechanism for unbounded composition, no way to represent “A entails B entails C” as a chain rather than a centroid. When a multi-hop query requires traversing such a chain, cosine similarity retrieves the centroid — typically the endpoint or a topically related distractor — and misses the intermediate steps. The VKG escapes this limitation by representing propositions as discrete nodes with explicit relational edges, restoring the structural information that the compression discards.

4.10 Semantic Loadedness: Visualizing the Compression Landscape

The dimensional cramming theory predicts that embedding space should exhibit a spectrum of semantic loadedness: some regions are semantically underloaded (generic, content-poor), while others are overloaded (too much information compressed into nearby vectors). To test this, we construct a 2D projection using two interpretable axes:

  • Gender axis (y): The normalized displacement vwoman − vman, capturing the gender direction in embedding space.
  • “Cute” modifier axis (x): The normalized displacement vcute woman − vwoman — the pure semantic contribution of adding “cute” as a modifier, without syntactic noise from articles or copulas.

The two axes are nearly orthogonal (dot product = −0.09), enabling meaningful 2D projection. We embed 194 items spanning twelve categories — gendered terms, cute entities, neutral/generic words, abstract concepts, nature, technical terms, actions, emotions, four types of propositions, and 54 intermediate phrases designed to test whether the transition between regions is smooth or cliff-like.

Voronoi tessellation. Rather than plotting concepts as points, we construct a Voronoi diagram where each concept owns a region — the set of all points closer to it than to any other concept. This mirrors how softmax partitions logit space into decision regions. Cell area directly encodes loadedness: large cells indicate underloaded regions (sparse, generic), small cells indicate overloaded regions (concepts crammed together).

Results. The gender axis separates cleanly (man at −0.44, woman at +0.44). The cute axis reveals the predicted loadedness spectrum:

  • Most overloaded (smallest cells): technical terms (mean area 0.000186), abstract concepts (0.000338), neutral propositions (0.000204) — dozens of concepts crammed into tiny slivers of the projection.
  • Mid-range: cute animals in isolation (puppy, kitten, duckling) and nature terms — moderate cell sizes with smooth transitions.
  • Most underloaded (largest cells): gendered terms (mean 0.018), “X is cute” propositions (mean 0.010), strength propositions (mean 0.005) — a few concepts owning vast territory.

The 54 intermediate phrases confirm that the transition between regions is completely smooth. The kitten gradient illustrates this: bare “kitten” (x = −0.01) → “the small fluffy kitten” (+0.08) → “kittens are cute” (+0.20) → “cute kitten” (+0.22) → “the cute kitten” (+0.23) → “a very cute kitten” (+0.26). No cliff anywhere — each step adds a small increment of semantic load along the cute axis. This confirms the prediction from §4.9: the compression landscape has smooth gradients, not sharp boundaries.

The area ratio between the most overloaded (wavelength: 0.000026) and most underloaded (gentleman: 0.084) cells is 3,200×. A single gendered word owns more projection territory than dozens of technical terms combined. This is dimensional cramming made visible: the embedding model allocates enormous representational capacity to high-signal concepts while compressing “less interesting” distinctions into negligible regions.

Three regimes of the embedding manifold. The loadedness spectrum is not merely a visualization artifact — it suggests three empirically distinguishable regimes, defined by symbolic discriminability (the degree to which local vector operations preserve predicate–argument structure), that govern what kind of reasoning the embedding space can support:

  • Undersymbolic space (large cells, sparse periphery): Regions where coordinates fail to support stable symbolic interpretations. Retrieval here tends to produce vague or under-specified results — the model has insufficient representational mass to anchor any specific concept. The boundary with the isosymbolic regime is gradual rather than sharp.
  • Isosymbolic space (moderate cells, coherent clusters): The regime where local vector arithmetic approximately preserves predicate–argument structure — where adjective modification acts as a consistent translation vector (§4.11), predicate embedding acts as an independent orthogonal transformation (§4.12), and compositionality is approximately additive. This is the regime where the “Vector Jump” works: a symbolic operation can be executed as vector arithmetic and the result decoded back into a valid symbol.
  • Oversymbolic space (tiny cells, the dense core): Regions where many symbolically distinct propositions are compressed into overlapping coordinates, so that vector operations cannot reliably distinguish propositions differing in predicate, role, or entailment structure. A small perturbation in vector coordinates can jump between semantically unrelated concepts, and retrieval from these regions is associated with hallucination — confident but logically incoherent outputs produced by stitching together fragments from multiple concepts that happen to share the same neighbourhood.

The transition between regimes is smooth (confirmed by the kitten gradient and the 54 intermediate phrases), but the qualitative consequences are sharp: the same vector operation (e.g., adding a predicate) that produces clean, interpretable displacement in the isosymbolic regime produces noise in the undersymbolic regime and semantic collisions in the oversymbolic regime. The 3,200× loadedness ratio between extremes suggests that the isosymbolic space is the smallest of the three regimes — a narrow manifold of reliable vector-symbol isomorphism surrounded by a vast undersymbolic void and a dense oversymbolic core.

Relation to hubness and polysemy. Two well-studied phenomena in high-dimensional embedding spaces — hubness and polysemy — superficially resemble the oversymbolic regime but are importantly distinct from it. Hubness is a statistical property of nearest-neighbour graphs in high dimensions: some points appear as nearest neighbours of many others due to the geometry of high-dimensional spaces, regardless of whether those neighbour relationships are semantically wrong. A hub can be a semantically central or generic concept that is genuinely related to many queries. Polysemy concerns lexical items with multiple senses — “bank” as riverbank vs. financial institution — and is a property of individual tokens, not of geometric regions.

The oversymbolic regime is a stricter and functionally different concept. We define it as a dense neighbourhood in which many symbolically distinct propositions — differing in predicates, argument roles, or truth conditions — are mapped to nearly indistinguishable embeddings, so that vector retrieval cannot reliably distinguish them. Hubs may correlate with oversymbolic regions, but hubness alone does not entail meaning-collapsing collisions: a point can be a frequent nearest neighbour without causing predicate or role confusion. Conversely, polysemy concerns the ambiguity of surface forms, whereas oversymbolic compression operates at the level of full propositional content — even unambiguous, compositionally transparent propositions can collide in oversymbolic regions because the embedding space lacks the capacity to separate them. The diagnostic criterion is not “does this point have many neighbours?” (hubness) or “does this word have multiple senses?” (polysemy), but “does this neighbourhood preserve symbolic discriminability — can vector operations here distinguish propositions that differ in predicate, role, or entailment structure?”

The undersymbolic/isosymbolic boundary, by contrast, is gradual rather than sharp. We observe a low-density regime in which embeddings fail to support stable symbolic readings, but its boundary with the isosymbolic regime is better understood as a smooth degradation of representational fidelity than as a discrete transition.

4.11 Large-Scale Validation: 500 Nouns × 5 Adjective Modifiers

To rule out sampling bias, we repeat the topology analysis with an unbiased, frequency-ranked sample: the 500 most common English nouns (from wordfreq), each embedded in six forms — bare noun plus five adjective-modified phrases (“powerful X”, “strong X”, “cute X”, “adorable X”, “beautiful X”) — yielding 3,000 total embeddings.

4.12 Two Dimensions of Complexity: Adjective vs. Predicate

The previous experiments projected onto axes defined by social dimensions (gender, cuteness). To test whether the topology generalizes to structural complexity, we define two new axes using a single anchor noun “Road”:

  • X-axis (adjective complexity): normalize(embed(“The Icy Road”) − embed(“Road”)) — the direction of adding a descriptive modifier
  • Y-axis (predicate complexity): normalize(embed(“Roads are Great”) − embed(“Road”)) — the direction of embedding the noun in a full predication

The two axes have dot product 0.27, indicating moderate but tolerable correlation. The same 500 most common English nouns are embedded in four forms: bare noun, “The Icy X” (adjective only), “Xs are Great” (predicate only), and “Icy Xs are Great” (both).

Additivity test. If adjective modification and predicate embedding are independent linear operations, “Icy Xs are Great” should project to approximately the sum of the adjective and predicate displacements. The reference point “Icy Roads are Great” lands at (0.648, 0.526) versus the additive prediction (0.720, 0.636), giving a ratio of 0.90 on X and 0.83 on Y. The combined transformation recovers ~85–90% of the expected displacement — sub-additive, but close enough to confirm approximate linearity. The shortfall is consistent with mild dimensional interference when two modifications compete for the same representational capacity.

This result strengthens the central thesis: symbolic operations (adjective modification, predication) map to approximately linear vector transformations, and these transformations are roughly orthogonal when they encode genuinely independent structural changes. The embedding space behaves as a compressed linear algebra over symbolic operations — but with non-negligible compression artifacts that accumulate under composition.

4.13 Specificity vs. Sentence Complexity

The previous experiments held sentence structure constant while varying the noun. Here we test both dimensions simultaneously: noun specificity (generic → proper noun) on the X-axis and sentence complexity (bare noun → complex predication) on the Y-axis. Ten semantic chains (Geography, Animals, Food, Cities, People, Vehicles, Buildings, Music, Astronomy, Nature) each traverse four levels of specificity (e.g., thing → place → mountain → Mount Everest) crossed with four levels of sentence complexity (bare noun, “I love X”, “I love X because they are great”, and an extended variant).

Three patterns emerge. First, sentence complexity saturates rapidly: the jump from bare noun to “I love X” accounts for most of the Y displacement (~0.4 units), while adding “because they are great” contributes only ~0.1 more, and extending the clause further adds almost nothing. The embedding model treats sentence structure as a coarse categorical signal, not a graded one. Second, specificity is compressed by sentence templates: bare nouns spread from X = 0.0 (“thing”) to X = 1.0 (“Mount Everest”), but once wrapped in “I love X because...”, all nouns compress to X = 0.3–0.5. The sentence template acts as an information bottleneck that discards noun identity. Third, the “simple” complexity line is nearly flat — “I love things”, “I love dogs”, and “I love Mount Everest” all project to Y ≈ 0.67, regardless of how different their nouns are.

This is precisely the failure mode that motivates the VKG: embedding models compress away noun identity when sentence structure takes over. A retrieval system that searches by vector similarity over sentence embeddings will treat “I love mountains because they are great” and “I love pizza because it is great” as near-neighbours — because the shared template dominates — while failing to connect “Mount Everest” (bare noun) with “I love Mount Everest because it is great” (sentence), despite referring to the same entity. The VKG solves this by extracting explicit entity mentions and linking them structurally, independent of the sentence template.

6. Discussion

6.1 The Extraction Bottleneck

Both losing scenarios (Bridge Collapse, Satellite Signal) trace to Pillar 1 failures: DeepSeek-R1 8B produces degenerate triples for multi-clause technical sentences. In our experiments, the VKG architecture does not underperform standard RAG when fed well-formed propositions. This suggests that extraction quality is the single bottleneck, and that a more capable extraction model would likely flip the losing scenarios without any changes to the graph or logic engine. Notably, 0 formal SPARQL-discovered chains were found in 2 of the 3 winning scenarios — entity bridging alone, without the logic engine, is doing the retrieval work. This points to an honest reframing: the current system is better described as “entity-bridged retrieval” than “logic-gated retrieval,” with the logic engine as a future capability that requires higher-quality inputs to activate.

6.2 Finite-State Machines and the Need for External Structure

As established in Section 2.3, a transformer with fixed parameters is formally a finite-state machine. Our embedding geometry experiments (Section 4) demonstrate the practical consequences of this theoretical limitation: the finite-dimensional vector space compresses propositional semantics with non-uniform fidelity, allocating representational capacity according to distributional signal strength rather than logical importance.

Crucially, embeddings do encode logical structure — our experiments show approximately additive composition of subject, predicate, and object, consistent verb displacement vectors, and systematic within-role clustering. The embedding space operates as a kind of compressed logical language, capable of representing many logical operations. But it is a finite-state logical language: it can translate certain logical relations (synonymy, topical similarity, entity association) but cannot perform unbounded compositional reasoning. The failure modes we observe — predicate under-representation, causal chain decay, the syllogism gap — are consequences of dimensional cramming: the non-uniform information loss when a finite-state system must compress an open logical space.

In Kahneman’s terms, the embedding space is System 1: fast, intuitive, associative, capable of rich pattern-matching. It excels at “this reminds me of that” but struggles with “if A then B, and B then C, therefore A then C” when the chain exceeds its fixed computational budget. The VKG adds System 2: slow, explicit, structural. The two systems are complementary, not competing — the embedding model identifies seed propositions, and the VKG traces the reasoning chains that connect them. In formal terms, the VKG is the external tape that promotes the finite-state embedding system toward Turing-completeness.

6.3 Tool Use vs. Architectural Integration

A common approach to overcoming LLM limitations is to provide external tools — calculators, code interpreters, search engines. While effective for specific tasks, tool use is fundamentally a prosthetic: it compensates for the model’s limitations without addressing the underlying representational gap. The model must still decide when to invoke a tool and how to interpret its output, both of which depend on the same vector-similarity intuitions that fail for multi-hop reasoning.

Our approach is architectural rather than tool-based. The VKG is not a tool the model calls; it is a parallel retrieval pathway that operates over explicit structure. The propositions, entities, and edges exist independently of the embedding space, allowing structural traversal to complement (and sometimes override) vector similarity. This distinction matters for reliability: tool use fails silently when the model doesn’t know what it doesn’t know, while the VKG surfaces structural connections regardless of whether the model would have thought to look for them.

6.4 Scalability and Efficiency

The neurosymbolic pipeline incurs a 16.9× time overhead compared to standard RAG, primarily from LLM-based proposition extraction. For offline or batch applications (legal discovery, scientific literature review, intelligence analysis), this overhead is acceptable. For real-time applications, two paths exist: (1) pre-computing VKGs for known corpora, amortizing extraction cost; (2) using faster or specialized extraction models (e.g., fine-tuned sequence-labeling models rather than general-purpose LLMs).

6.5 The Three Regimes and Two Directions of Neurosymbolic Computation

The undersymbolic/isosymbolic/oversymbolic partition (§4.10) is not merely a descriptive taxonomy — it implies two complementary computational strategies, each exploiting the isosymbolic manifold from a different direction.

Neural → Symbolic → Neural (hallucination prevention). This is the direction implemented by our VKG architecture. The embedding model performs initial retrieval (neural), the VKG imposes structural constraints through entity bridging and chain search (symbolic), and the validated reasoning chain is returned to the generation model (neural). The symbolic layer acts as a filter that prevents the system from operating in oversymbolic regions where vector arithmetic produces hallucinations. When the embedding space is too dense for reliable vector operations, the graph provides rigid, unambiguous edges that the manifold cannot. This is the application demonstrated in our benchmark: the VKG recovers chain steps that cosine similarity misses precisely because those steps fall in oversymbolic or undersymbolic regions relative to the query.

Symbolic → Neural → Symbolic (compute acceleration). The converse direction is equally significant but less explored. When a complex logical operation falls entirely within the isosymbolic manifold — where vector transformations are isomorphic to symbol manipulations — it can be executed as vector arithmetic rather than graph traversal. Consider a knowledge graph query requiring a 5-hop traversal with branching factor k: the symbolic cost is O(k5). If the start and end points of the traversal both lie in the isosymbolic space, a single vector operation (translate by the “relationship vector”) could approximate the same result in O(1). The symbolic system enters the neural manifold, performs constant-time vector arithmetic, and exits back into the symbolic graph at the destination node — a “latent wormhole” that short-circuits expensive graph traversal.

Our embedding geometry experiments provide initial evidence that such jumps are feasible: adjective modification, predicate embedding, and entity-role transformations all behave as approximately linear operations within the isosymbolic regime (§§4.11–4.12). The key constraint is that both the entry and exit coordinates must lie within the isosymbolic manifold — jumps that land in oversymbolic space will produce collisions (multiple candidate nodes), and jumps that land in undersymbolic space will fail to resolve to any node at all.

The mapping problem. Both applications depend on the same prerequisite: a system that can predetermine which regime a coordinate occupies before committing to a reasoning strategy. Such a “topological classifier” would enable an AI system to route queries adaptively — using fast vector arithmetic in isosymbolic regions, switching to explicit symbolic reasoning in oversymbolic regions, and flagging undersymbolic regions as requiring more information before reasoning can proceed. Local metrics such as nearest-neighbour density, local intrinsic dimensionality, and eigenvalue spread of the local covariance matrix are candidate signals for this classification. If the boundaries of the three regimes can be mapped reliably, the result is not just a retrieval improvement but a reliability map for the entire embedding space — a system that knows where its own vector math can be trusted.

Implications for knowledge graph architecture. In this view, the future of knowledge graphs is not a choice between symbolic and neural representations but a cache-inference duality. The isosymbolic manifold acts as an infinite virtual knowledge graph — capable of generating edges on the fly through vector arithmetic — while the physical knowledge graph serves two irreducible functions: entity resolution (anchoring continuous coordinates to discrete identities, which the manifold alone cannot guarantee) and oversymbolic decompression (providing rigid symbolic scaffolding in regions where the manifold is too dense for reliable vector operations). The physical graph is not the source of truth; it is a high-fidelity cache for the most frequently needed and most reliability-critical fragments of an implicitly infinite relational space.

6.6 Future Directions

Ablation study. Our data already suggests that entity bridging alone (Pillar 2, without the logic engine) accounts for most of the gains. A formal ablation — bridging-only vs. full pipeline — would clarify the marginal contribution of formal reasoning.

Differentiable logic. The hard boundary between the neural and symbolic components is a potential limitation. Recent work on differentiable logic (t-norm fuzzy logic layers, deep probabilistic programming) offers a path toward bridging the two representations — allowing gradient-based learning to flow through symbolic structures. This could enable end-to-end training of extraction and reasoning jointly, rather than treating them as independent modules.

Cross-domain structural analogy. The VKG makes relational structure explicit. Two domains with isomorphic causal graphs (e.g., pharmacological inhibition and economic supply disruption) would produce isomorphic VKG subgraphs, enabling cross-domain analogical reasoning. This is impossible in pure embedding space, where identical predicates across domains are nearly invisible (§4.4).

7. Conclusion

Embedding spaces encode logical structure — but with systematically non-uniform fidelity, compressing predicate and relational information while amplifying entity and topical information. We have shown this empirically across six probes of embedding geometry: subjects dominate predicates by 3.5×, syllogism premises are the most distant pair in their embedding neighborhood, causal chain endpoints decay to near-random similarity, identical predicates across domains score barely above chance, no universal abstraction axis exists, and even Linnaean taxonomy cannot be recovered from distributional geometry. These are not model-specific artifacts — they are consequences of compressing an open logical space into a finite-state representation (Section 2.3) optimized for distributional co-occurrence rather than logical entailment.

The Virtual Knowledge Graph addresses this by restoring what the compression discards: explicit entities, explicit predicates, and explicit edges. On a 7-scenario benchmark, this structural augmentation yields 69% mean ground-truth coverage versus 62% for standard RAG, with the largest gains (+50%) on causal cascade scenarios where intermediate chain steps have low query similarity but share entities with adjacent steps. The two scenarios where standard RAG wins are attributable to extraction model failures rather than architectural limitations.

The current system is best understood as entity-bridged retrieval: the VKG’s primary contribution is connecting propositions through shared extracted entities, creating paths that cosine similarity cannot. The logic engine (contradiction detection, formal chain search) is architecturally present but empirically underutilized, awaiting higher-quality extraction to activate. The bottleneck is Pillar 1, not the graph.

More broadly, our embedding geometry experiments suggest that the latent space is not a uniform representational medium but a heterogeneous manifold exhibiting three empirically distinguishable regimes — defined not by density alone but by symbolic discriminability: the undersymbolic space (sparse, where embeddings fail to support stable symbolic readings), the isosymbolic space (where local vector operations approximately preserve predicate–argument structure), and the oversymbolic space (dense, where many symbolically distinct propositions collapse into overlapping coordinates). This characterization is distinct from classical hubness (frequent nearest neighbours that need not involve meaning collisions) and polysemy (lexical ambiguity of surface forms). The three-regime view suggests two complementary applications: neural → symbolic → neural reasoning (our VKG, which grounds retrieval in explicit structure) and, as a design implication we have not yet implemented, symbolic → neural → symbolic shortcuts (where complex graph traversals could in principle be executed as vector arithmetic within the isosymbolic regime). A system that can map these regime boundaries — predetermining where its own vector math is reliable — would not only improve retrieval but provide a reliability map for the entire embedding space.

The embedding space is the fast, intuitive map; the VKG is the slow, precise road network. Together, they navigate terrain that neither can cross alone.
